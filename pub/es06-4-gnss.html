Operational Principles

Space Vehicles

Flying Clocks

  • On-board atomic clock synchronized to GPS Time
  • Deviation between satellites \( \lt 1ns\)
  • Modulates ranging code on to carrier
  • Modulates data code on to ranging code
  • Ranging code has known alignment in time
Known time alignment means that when you receive the ranging code, you can uniquely work out the time at which it was sent. By comparing that to the time at the receiver, you can work out how far the signal has travelled and therefore the distance to the satellite.
  • Bi-Siderial Orbit
  • Satellites broadcast Ephemeris data describing their orbits
  • Travel at \(\approx 14000km/h\) with position known to \(\approx 1m\) at all times
The Bi-Siderial orbit means that the same satellites are visible in the same location at the same time every day. Modulation

The ranging code is a Gold Code: A pseudo-random sequence that doesn't repeat for a very long time. The phase of this code is flipped depending on the value of the data stream. At the receiving end, the ranging code is retrieved; its position (in time) gives the distance to the satellite and its phase gives the value of one bit in the data stream. The modified ranging code is similarly modulated on to a carrier wave by flipping the phase of the carrier wave. The frequency of the carrier wave is a little over 1.5GHz for the primary civilian frequency (L1), ~1.2GHz for the L2 frequency used in high-end and military receivers.

Ground Segment

  • Monitors all the satellites
  • Maintains the reference clock
  • Computes any drift in satellite clocks

Receivers

  • Receives ranging signals from \(\ge 4\) satellites, called pseudoranges
  • Uses satellite positions and pseudorange measurements to solve for receiver position in time and space
\[r = \left[ X, Y, Z, T \right]^T \] or \[r = \left[ Lat, Lon, Alt, T \right]^T \]

By a process called Trilateration

Trilateration

In the GPS case, we are actually solving in four dimensions and therefore this technique should possibly be called quadlateration.

Sources of Error

Satellite Clock Errors

  • \(1 ns\) synchronization between satellites is still \(\approx 30cm\)
  • The satellites are moving quickly and experience less gravity

General Relativity!

Clocks on the satellites loose \(\approx 38\mu S = 11km \) per day relative to clocks on the ground.

Receiver Clocks

Time at the receiver is solved, just like position. Normal oscillators drift about \(100ns = 3m\) each second. Fitting receivers with atomic clocks is not feasible!

Orbital Errors

Satellites are \(20,000km\) away moving at \(14000km/h\). The data sent from the satellite describing its orbit is limited in precision. As such, the accuracy to which we know the position of a satellite is only a few metres.

Multipath

Obstructions and reflections combine to give longer-than-truth flight paths.

Atmospheric Errors

The Ionosphere is electrically-charged and interferes with the signal propagation.

The Troposphere is humid and varying temperatures; these change the air's refractive index.

These both serve to increase the distance that the signal must travel. Typically, the error at the ground is around 10's of metres.

Correction Methods

Differential GPS

Receivers near each other will see approximately the same errors.

  • Same Atmosphere above them
  • Same satellite position errors
  • Same satellite clock errors

A receiver with known position can measure these errors and send them to nearby mobile receivers.

The reference receiver will have a known position in space, not in time. It will still need to estimate the time at its location and any error in this estimation will stay in the system.

Post-Processing

The calculated errors may be stored, rather than transmitted, and the moving GPS can have their position “fixed” later. This is useful for post-activity analysis.

SBAS

Sometimes the errors can be transmitted back to the satellites, then back to roving receivers. These are called Satellite-Based Augmentation Systems (SBAS). SBAS cover the USA, Europe and Japan.
Sometimes here in Australia, our receivers “accidentally” see SBAS signals from Japan. The Japanese signals are from so far away, and therefore so different to what we need, that they often act to make our position solutions worse. It is often prudent to disable SBAS on receivers in areas without formal coverage, such as Australia.

Real-Time Kinematic

By tracking the phase of the carrier signal, the position estimate can be refined (up to an integer ambiguity).
This is a simplification as phase is only a relative measurement and we do not know the phase of the signal at the satellite. As such, a second, fixed, receiver is used (like in DGPS) and the phase measurements are compared between that fixed receiver and the moveable one.

Multi-frequency

The major errors are caused by atmospheric effects. These effects affect different frequencies in different ways.

High-end receivers track multiple frequencies from the GPS satellites and use all the measurements, along with atmospheric models, to work out and subtract the atmospheric effects without requiring a second, stationary receiver.

AGPS

Assisted GPS is a name for a number of related technologies, where information about the state of the GPS system is sent to receivers through secondary channels (e.g. the data connection of a phone).

This data can include correction data like DGPS, but also the current time and location of the satellites in order to speed up aquisition (time to first fix).
